Ethos

Control
This was, is, and will always be my answer as to why I decided to quit social media and make a website. This tells you why I started to step outside the walled gardens of socmed, and the other ethos tell you why I continued doing it. Using socmed is a gradual process of losing control - over your screentime, your activity and purchase habit, , over your brain hormones, your mood and online behavior, over your privacy and your very identity. It all happens in the ambience and it's 100% legal.
DIY Punk
Punk culture was all about doing things yourself, which meant you had to learn how things were done in the first place. The punks strive to build, create, and contribute as much as they consume. They broke down tools and dismantled ideas, for the sake of reverse-engineering and learning the history of stuff. They have a knack for getting their point accross in creative and outrageous ways. They were NIMBYs, Luddites, romantics, standing in the way of progress.
Brutalism
Build the website from scratch with vanilla HTML/CSS. External libraries (like JQuery, Bootstrap...), preprocessors (like SASS, LESS...), and content managing platforms (like WordPress, Wix, Tumblr...) always come with a bunch of bells and whistles or way more functionality than needed for a simple, personal web page. Refuse to use them, take pride in writing YOUR own code, return to basic and build the most out of the least.
Sustainability
Build the website to last. Offline first, static first, native first, and as stated above, avoid dependency on cloud resources. Stick to W3C standards. Use the same URL to combat link rot, and maintain the website. Make the website as ligthweight as possible, and thereby play nice with old devices, slow internet, and disabilities.
Freedom
No tracking and other shenanigans, avoid linking/embedding resources from sites that track users (Gaggle, Facebonk, etc). Stick to the ethos of Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) and allow users to copy/distribute resources under fair use instead of gatekeeping them.

03/12/21

Changed the font to Arial. Good ol' sans-serif Arial. At first I didn't expect to do a lot of writing so this whole page was set in Menlo, Roboto Mono or monospace (there was a lot of ASCII art around here so setting all the texts to monospace saved me a lot of formatting). But after some long ass blog (rant) entries I realized that:

  1. Monospace fonts make it easier to recognize letters, but not words.
  2. Serif fonts make it easier to recognize words, but not letters. Also they give me visual fatigue.
  3. Sans-serif fonts are perfect for reading, and Arial is the best sans-serif font ever made.

A bit of letter-spacing and line-height magic and Arial becomes the most readable font ever.

Readable monospaced fonts like Monaco is on thin ice (and it's also used in the code editor of Neocities I believe?). But hey Monaco isn't one of the default fonts in some browsers. So, boo.

Courier should be used very sparingly for it is a reading nightmare, as it is both monospaced AND serif. I get tired looking at all the nooks and crannies of it and if I need to read a wall of text in Courier I'll just hang myself.

That said, if the text was formatted properly (i.e it has hierarchy and moderate paragraph spacing, unlike mine) and if the content was well-written (unlike mine), then I honestly don't mind reading it in Times New Roman.
